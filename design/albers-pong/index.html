Albers Pong

Albers Pong November 22, 2017

The Albers Principle states that a color’s communication is affected by its relationship to other colors. With this principle in mind, enter project four of Design 210 - Color and Composition. The goals for the project were to use motion and/or interaction to clearly prove the Albers Principle. and ensure that the smoosh(s) and concept should feel naturally integrated with each other.

Challenges & Process

This was an exciting opportunity to build anything I could imagine and be unique in my solution. And once I started, I dreamt big. Huge animations, interactive iPad books (turns out it already exists), and then it hit me. I would create a new school twist on an old-school video game. I considered a lot of games but the simplicity of Pong was gravitating—and plausible.

I planned it all out. I would take a standard pong game and add colors. On each half of the screen, the background colors would match the Albers Principle. Then the paddles and ball would all be the same color and as you played the game you would slowly begin to understand the principle. After discussing the idea further I realized that, while this was a great starting point, there was certainly still a gap between my game idea and a concept that successfully integrates the Albers Principle, and so I got to work.

My initial brainstorm for the project

Through critiques and conversations with classmates, I came up with an additional gameplay mechanic that would take my concept from pong with color to its own standalone game. Along with the up and down gameplay that pong is famous for, Albers Pong would use your left and right arrows (or A and D) to change the color of your paddle. This twist helped me break away from standard pong and better integrate the Albers Principle into my game. I then added a power-up that spikes the ball when you have the correct paddle color, and a punishment that shrinks your paddle does not match the ball color.

Once I had the gameplay planned out and designed, I set out to code a working version of Albers Pong. As someone with a brief understanding of HTML and CSS and no Javascript experience, this was certainly an intimidating task. As soon as I decided to make a pong game I began taking online lessons in Javascript as to better understand the base code of Javascript pong. With this new knowledge, I began coding my own version of javascript pong, slowly adding the components to bring the game to reality.

A demo of the gameplay

Takeaways

This project was a vast learning experience for me. It was my first time coding in Javascript, and between the Codecademy lessons I took and all of the documentation I read, I gained a lot of knowledge and confidence in Front-End Web Development. I also learned how useful todo lists can be when working on large projects with a lot of moving parts, I will certainly continue to use such lists in my design process.

Aside from the actual coding and development, this project helped me understand what it means to resolve your designs. It is the final step in any process that really polishes your design. It's about all the little details that make the solution feel wholesome and thoroughly designed. For Albers Pong, I added a start screen and win screen to take the game from a random design project hosted on my website, to a truly standalone game.
