Visualizing Emotion

Visualizing Emotion December 13, 2017

As my final project for Design 210 - Color and Composition, we were tasked with conveying the emotions of 2 movies/albums by the same director/artist, as dynamic compositions. I decided to create compositions for The Dark Knight and The Prestige by Christopher Nolan.

Challenges & Process

Part of the project required that the two compositions feel visually distinct from each other, yet when the compositions are side by side should feel like part of the same set. This idea, along with the limitation of using solely abstract imagery to convey such complex emotions was the biggest challenge for me.

After discussing with those doing the same movies, we agreed on Adamant and Inflamed as our emotions for The Dark Knight, and Vulnerable and Agonised for The Prestige. These words are not simply defined and so my first task was to gain a deeper understanding of what each word meant and the types of imagery and color they conjure up. Over the course of the next few days, I researched each emotion, flipped through color schemes, and even sketched rapidly alongside the scenes to ideate. Being able to understand the emotions on a deeper level gave me the confidence to experiment with my compositions more.

Throughout the entire project, I would constantly check back in and ask why I was making specific decisions and how they were being used to convey each emotion. This helped keep my designs on track and have a better understanding of each decision I made. By the end of the project, I was ready the reflect on each emotion individually. Below are excerpts from that reflection.

The Dark Knight

To convey adamant, I created forceful movement throughout the composition using red streaks. These streaks create a strong texture that helps move your eyes around the composition and convey direction of the lines. I chose red for these streaks to help intertwine the emotion with the motivation behind it.

As I began to build my composition I found shattered glass to be an extremely effective way to convey inflamed. With rage, things are often broken and as Batman becomes angrier, he throws The Joker against a wall of glass. I almost think of the broken glass in the composition to be the bits that fall in that scene. Adding drops of "blood" to the glass helps tie it into the composition while further displaying the idea of inflamed and destructive actions.

The Prestige

Using both black paint and dripping mascara, I built a composition to convey vulnerability. Thinking about this emotion in a concrete sense helped influence my decisions to have the tear like forms fall down the composition into these black voids that have been ripped open. These voids become an added layer of depth in the complex emotions of the composition, as though the true, most vulnerable self is now being ripped open.

When I thought of the pain and suffering in the scene, I began to think of dark, dull red tones. This helped me design the base of the composition, where we see painful agony (red) slowly covering what was once a white, pure, and innocent soul. This rough red texture, aggressively filling the composition also helped introduce another color to the composition. Lastly, I tied the composition together using the forms created by the dripping tears and splattered bits of black. While crying is often considered weak and vulnerable, recognizing that the cause of the tears, in this case, was the pain and agony help bring these two individual emotions together.

Takeaways

One of the biggest things I learned from this project was how to create my own limitations and build upon the success of earlier iterations. As far as Color and Comp projects go, this was one of the most open-ended assignments we had, something that our whole class was excited for until we realized that when had no clue what we were doing. In class, we talked about how we can create our own limitations by narrowing in on what is working in the composition and building from there. This certainly evolved my design process as I was no longer trying to create hundreds of new concepts, instead, I was able to iterate through versions of one idea.

Lastly, this project helped me understand the importance of self-critique as I began to take the time to ask why I was making certain decisions, and considering how successful those decisions were in relation to the project goals.
